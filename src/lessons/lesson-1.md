# Getting Started with Tarot
This lesson gives a compact, practical introduction to Tarot so you can begin learning with clarity and confidence. Topics covered:

- History of Tarot
- What Tarot is — and what it isn't
- The four suits and what they represent
- Choosing a deck
- The Rider–Waite (Rider–Waite–Smith) tradition
- A short practice plan to get started

## 1. A brief history of Tarot

Tarot cards originated in 15th-century Europe as playing cards. Over the centuries they were adapted for esoteric and divinatory uses. The modern familiar form — 22 Major Arcana and 56 Minor Arcana divided into four suits — became widespread in the 18th–20th centuries. The Rider–Waite–Smith deck (published 1909) popularized imagery and symbolic details that many contemporary decks follow or reference.

## 2. What Tarot is — and what it isn't

- Tarot is a symbolic language: images, numbers, and suits provide metaphors for psychological states, life events, and patterns.
- Tarot is a tool for reflection, storytelling, and sense-making; it helps people notice possibilities and perspectives.
- Tarot is not a deterministic machine that predicts fixed futures, nor is it a substitute for professional advice (medical, legal, financial).

Approach readings as prompts for insight, not absolute answers.

## 3. The four suits (quick guide)

- Wands (also called Rods, Staves): action, creativity, will, projects and energy.
- Cups: emotions, relationships, intuition, matters of the heart.
- Swords: thought, communication, conflict, decisions and mental clarity.
- Pentacles (Coins, Disks): material world, work, finances, body and practical matters.

Each suit contains pip cards (Ace through Ten) that describe everyday dynamics and four court cards (Page, Knight, Queen, King) that often represent people, roles, or approaches.

## 4. Choosing your deck

- Start with imagery that speaks to you: if the artwork helps you notice symbols and feelings, it's a good fit.
- Beginner-friendly decks often use clear symbolic scenes (Rider–Waite-style decks are common recommendations).
- Consider factors like size, card finish, and cultural/ethical sensitivity (some decks borrow imagery from living traditions—choose respectfully).

Try borrowing or viewing several decks online before buying. Your first deck should invite practice, not intimidate it.

## 5. The Rider–Waite (Rider–Waite–Smith) tradition

Designed by A. E. Waite with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith and published by Rider in 1909, this deck emphasizes evocative scenes on the pip cards and clear symbolic cues on the Majors. Because many modern interpretations and teaching materials reference Rider–Waite imagery, it's a useful study reference even if you later prefer a different artistic style.

## 6. Getting started — a simple practice plan

1. Become familiar: Spend a week looking at the deck. Shuffle, fan, and simply view each card. Note which images draw your attention.
2. Daily card: Pull one card each morning. Write three words or a sentence about what you notice. Do this for 7–14 days to build visual memory.
3. Short spread: Learn a 3-card spread (Past / Present / Next steps). Ask a simple question and place three cards left-to-right. Record one-sentence meanings.
4. Journal: Keep notes on repeated symbols, emotional reactions, and connections to your life. Use the Notes feature on each card to capture ideas.
